## Braising

### What It Is
Braising is moist-heat cooking in a covered vessel with a small amount of liquid. It is the best technique for tough, collagen-rich cuts that need time to break down.

### The Science
Collagen (the connective tissue in tougher cuts) hydrolyzes into gelatin at 160–180°F when exposed to sustained moist heat over 2–4 hours. Gelatin dissolves into the braising liquid, creating richness and body. Muscle fibers relax at 200–210°F (the internal temperature inside a covered 325°F oven), becoming pullable and tender. Higher temperatures tighten muscle fibers — which is why a braised piece of meat cooked at 400°F is tough and dry.

### The Complete Process
1. **Choose the right cut**: chuck roast, short ribs, lamb shoulder, beef shank, chicken thighs. These cuts have connective tissue. Lean cuts (sirloin, chicken breast) do not respond well to braising.
2. **Pat bone dry**: surface moisture steams instead of browns. The protein surface must be above 280°F to trigger Maillard reactions.
3. **Sear in batches**: heat a Dutch oven over high heat until avocado oil or tallow shimmers. Add protein in a single layer with space between pieces. 90 seconds to 2 minutes per side. Remove and repeat.
4. **Build aromatics**: lower heat, add diced onion, carrots, celery. Cook until softened (5 min). Add garlic and spices; bloom 1 minute.
5. **Deglaze**: add wine, beer, stock, or a combination. Use a wooden spoon to scrape every bit of fond from the pan bottom. The fond dissolves and concentrates into the liquid.
6. **Combine and submerge**: return protein to pot. Add liquid to come two-thirds up the sides. Do not fully submerge — the top exposed to steam is part of the cooking.
7. **Braise low and slow**: cover tightly. Transfer to 325°F oven (or maintain a very gentle simmer on the stovetop). Cook until fork-tender: 2–3 hours for chuck, 3–4 hours for shanks or oxtail.
8. **Finish**: remove protein, strain and reduce the braising liquid if desired, skim fat, adjust seasoning.

### Troubleshooting
- **Meat is tough after 2 hours**: Not done yet — continue cooking. Check that oven is actually at 325°F.
- **Braising liquid is watery**: Uncover and reduce on the stovetop. Or remove protein and reduce to sauce consistency.
- **Meat is dry**: Oven was too hot. Reduce to 300°F next time. Or meat was overcooked past the gelatin sweet spot (very rare in home cooking, but possible with very lean cuts like brisket flat).
- **No fond development**: Meat was wet, pan was too cold, or pan was too crowded. Each issue prevents browning.

## Stock Making

### Chicken Stock
**Yield**: 2–3 quarts from one carcass

1. Place the carcass (with any attached meat and skin) in a pot. Add cold water to cover by 2 inches.
2. Bring slowly to a gentle simmer. As it heats, white foam will rise — skim it off with a spoon. This is coagulated protein; removing it produces clearer stock.
3. Add aromatics after skimming: 1 halved onion, 2 carrots, 2 celery stalks, 10 peppercorns, 2 bay leaves, a few thyme sprigs.
4. Maintain a **very gentle simmer** (180–190°F, small lazy bubbles). Never boil. Boiling causes proteins to break into tiny particles and emulsify into the stock, making it cloudy and slightly bitter.
5. Simmer 3–4 hours for chicken. Do not stir.
6. Strain through a fine-mesh strainer. Discard solids.
7. Cool rapidly (ice bath in the sink). Refrigerate.
8. The next day, the stock will be gelled (good — this is the gelatin) and have a layer of fat on top. Remove fat for clear stock, or stir it in for richer flavor.

**For bone broth**: Use raw bones (roasted first at 400°F for 20 min for flavor), add 2 tbsp apple cider vinegar, and simmer 12–24 hours. The acid helps extract minerals. Strain and season.

### Beef Stock
Same process as chicken but:
- Roast bones at 425°F until deeply browned (30–40 min) before simmering — this develops flavor through the Maillard reaction
- Simmer 6–8 hours minimum
- The stock will be darker, richer, and more intensely flavored

### Storage
- Refrigerator: 5 days
- Freezer: 6 months
- Freeze in 1-cup and 2-cup portions in zip bags laid flat

## Bread Baking: Yeast Doughs

### The Ingredients and Their Roles
- **Flour**: provides structure through gluten development
- **Water**: hydrates gluten proteins; controls crumb texture (more water = larger holes)
- **Salt**: strengthens gluten, controls fermentation speed, adds flavor
- **Yeast**: ferments sugars → CO2 (leavening) + flavor compounds
- **Fat** (butter, avocado oil): coats gluten strands, tenderizes, adds richness
- **Sugar**: feeds yeast, promotes browning, adds sweetness

### Fermentation Variables
| Temperature | Effect |
|-------------|--------|
| 40°F (fridge) | Extremely slow — 8–16+ hours; develops complex flavor |
| 65°F | Slow — 3–4 hours bulk fermentation |
| 75–80°F | Standard — 1–2 hours bulk fermentation |
| 90°F | Fast — 30–45 minutes; less flavor development |
| 110°F+ | Kills yeast |

### The Windowpane Test
Pinch off a golf-ball piece of dough. Stretch slowly with both hands, rotating. Well-developed dough stretches to a translucent membrane without tearing. If it tears immediately, knead 2 more minutes and test again.

### Shaping Tension
Surface tension in a shaped loaf or roll contains the CO2 from fermentation and gives structure. Build tension by dragging the shaped dough across an unfloured surface while cupping it with your hand. The friction grips the bottom and pulls the surface tighter.

### Common Bread Problems
- **Dense, heavy crumb**: under-fermented, insufficient gluten development, or too much flour added during kneading
- **Gummy interior**: under-baked; bake to 200–210°F internal temperature
- **Flat loaves**: over-fermented (yeast exhausted), dead yeast, or insufficient shaping tension
- **Thick tough crust**: too much steam in oven, or oven temperature too low
- **Overly open crumb (good for sourdough, bad for rolls)**: very high hydration + active fermentation

## Fat Rendering

### Tallow from Beef Suet
Suet is the hard fat surrounding the kidneys — highest quality tallow source. Fat trimmings from other cuts also work.

1. Request fat trimmings from a butcher when buying beef. Often given free or at very low cost.
2. Trim away any meat or connective tissue from the fat.
3. Grind or finely chop the fat. Smaller pieces render faster and more completely.
4. Place in a heavy pot or Dutch oven on the lowest possible heat setting, or in a 250°F oven.
5. The fat will slowly melt and release liquid fat, leaving behind crackling (crispy protein solids).
6. After 2–4 hours, the fat is fully liquid and the crackling is golden and crispy.
7. Strain through cheesecloth while still hot. The crackling is edible — season with salt.
8. Pour into clean glass jars. It will solidify to ivory-white when cool.
9. Store: room temperature for 6–12 months if well-rendered; refrigerator for 2+ years.

**Quality indicator**: Pure white, firm, mild clean smell = good render. Yellow, soft, or off smell = fat was overheated or contaminated with meat.

### Ghee from Butter
1. Use unsalted butter. Start with 1 lb (4 sticks).
2. Melt in a medium saucepan over medium-low heat.
3. First stage: butter foams as water evaporates. Stir occasionally.
4. Second stage: foam subsides. Milk solids sink to the bottom and begin to brown.
5. Third stage: the ghee is clear amber, and the bottom solids are golden-brown (not dark brown).
6. Remove immediately from heat — the solids continue cooking in the residual heat.
7. Strain through cheesecloth or a coffee filter into a clean glass jar.
8. Store at room temperature for 3–6 months, or refrigerate for up to 1 year.

**Warning signs**: Dark brown solids = slightly burnt ghee (still usable but slightly nutty/bitter). Black solids = burnt, discard and start over.

## Lacto-Fermentation

### How It Works
Salt creates an environment where lactic acid bacteria (LAB) thrive and pathogenic bacteria cannot survive. LAB are naturally present on vegetables. They convert sugars to lactic acid, lowering the pH (making the environment acidic), which preserves the food and creates the characteristic tangy flavor.

### Two Methods

**Brine fermentation** (for whole or cut vegetables):
1. Prepare a 2–3% brine: 1–2 tbsp non-iodized salt per 2 cups water. Dissolve completely.
2. Pack vegetables tightly into a clean glass jar with any flavorings (garlic, dill, peppercorns).
3. Pour brine to cover. Leave 1 inch headspace.
4. Weight vegetables to keep below brine surface — exposure to air risks mold.
5. Cover loosely (cloth, loose lid) to allow CO2 to escape.
6. Ferment at room temperature (65–75°F) for 3–7 days. Taste daily starting day 3.
7. When pleasantly sour: move to refrigerator to slow fermentation.

**Dry-salt fermentation** (for sauerkraut, kimchi):
1. Shred or slice vegetables finely.
2. Add 1–2% salt by weight (roughly 1 tsp per cup of shredded cabbage).
3. Massage vigorously until the vegetables release their own liquid (brine).
4. Pack tightly into a jar, pressing until vegetables are submerged in their own brine.
5. Weight down and ferment as above.

### What's Normal vs. What's Wrong
**Normal**: cloudy brine, bubbling, sour smell, slight effervescence, white sediment at bottom
**Problem**: pink or red brine, black or dark mold on surface, slimy texture, distinctly unpleasant (vs. just sour) smell → discard

### Timeline by Temperature
| Temperature | Fermentation Speed | Flavor |
|-------------|-------------------|--------|
| 60–65°F | Slow (10–14 days) | Complex, nuanced |
| 70–75°F | Standard (5–7 days) | Good balance |
| 80°F+ | Fast (2–4 days) | Less complex |

## Cast Iron Care

### Why Cast Iron
Exceptional heat retention — critical for searing. Heavy pan maintains temperature when cold food hits hot metal; thinner pans drop dramatically. Naturally non-stick when well-seasoned. Lasts generations.

### Seasoning Chemistry
Thin layers of fat applied and then heated above the smoke point cause the fat molecules to polymerize — link together into a hard, smooth, plastic-like coating. This coating is both non-stick and protective against rust. Thick layers don't polymerize uniformly and remain sticky.

### Maintenance Protocol After Every Use
1. While still warm, use a paper towel or brush to wipe out food
2. If needed, rinse with hot water (no soap)
3. Dry immediately over medium heat on the stovetop (30 seconds)
4. Apply a paper-towel-thin coat of avocado oil, tallow, or flaxseed oil to all surfaces
5. Store dry

### Restoring Neglected or Rusty Cast Iron
1. Scrub rust with steel wool or a chain mail scrubber and hot soapy water
2. Rinse and dry completely in a 200°F oven
3. Apply thin coat of oil and bake at 500°F for 1 hour
4. Cool in oven; repeat 3–5 times

### What Damages Seasoning
- Soaking in water (causes rust)
- Leaving wet (causes rust)
- Cooking acidic foods (tomatoes, wine, citrus) for extended periods in an unseasoned pan — acid strips seasoning
- Very high heat with no oil (strips and burns off seasoning)
